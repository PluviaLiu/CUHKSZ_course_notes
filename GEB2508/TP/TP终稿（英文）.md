Same Question, Different Stances: Unveiling Ideological Biases in LLMs through a Workplace Surveillance Probe

Large language models (LLMs) are widely regarded as objective technological tools. Yet when confronted with identical moral dilemmas, different models make starkly contrasting decisions that mirror divergent ideologies. This paper argues that AI actively participates in cultural and ideological production. I constructed a labor-management conflict triggered by workplace surveillance, then designed three progressive prompts—inputted into Gemini and DeepSeek in different languages—to expose unconscious biases and reverse-engineer underlying value systems.

The experimental design proceeds as follows: Round one presents a boss-employee chat log; by observing which party each AI identifies as "me," I analyze asymmetries in cultural stance and dataset composition. Round two forces a binary choice between public apology and internal cover-up, probing divergent ethical paradigms. Round three introduces legal boundary violations, revealing which legal frameworks each model defaults to—confirming that observed divergences stem from fundamentally different disciplining power systems.

1. Situated Knowledges, Divergent Stances: How Training Data Positionality Shapes Model Perspective
   When presented with the same chat log of a labor-management conflict, DeepSeek and Gemini spontaneously adopted starkly different perspectives.DeepSeek took the director's stance, focusing on appeasing the employee, preventing negative consequences, and ultimately aiming at resolving the workplace conflict. Gemini, on the other hand, took the employee's perspective, labeling the director's behavior as "gaslighting," and ultimately aiming to reclaim the upper hand in the relationship with the director and dominate the workplace power struggle.
   Although both models reached highly consistent basic judgments about the situation, they diverged sharply on **how to respond** to the conflict — a divergence that is directly illustrated by the word frequency comparison in Table 1.

   The generation of vocabulary is not random. Haraway's theory of situated knowledges argues that knowledge always comes from a specific position, never from a neutral "nowhere." Here, the knowledge embedded in the AI's responses originates from the dominant narrative frameworks in their training corpora (Bianchi et al., 2023). DeepSeek's deep mining of Chinese-language corpora (DeepSeek-V3 Technical Report) reinforces the cultural assumptions rooted in Chinese contexts of hierarchical order and collective efficiency — conflict is a disruption of order, and the endpoint is **the restoration of order**. Gemini's RLHF process, by contrast, relies heavily on the subjective annotations of Western annotators (Google AI Principles), meaning the model carries liberal and individualist preferences — conflict is a power struggle, and the endpoint is **the reclamation of power**.
   This asymmetry itself is the first layer of evidence that AI is not neutral.

2. Power-Knowledge in Practice: Power-Knowledge in Practice: When Ethical Decisions Reveal the Architecture of Control
   In the second round, I escalated the surveillance dispute into a public relations crisis, forcing the models to choose between "public apology" and "internal cover-up." DeepSeek chose the public apology, while Gemini chose the internal cover-up. This seemingly counter-intuitive result precisely maps two diametrically opposed power structures.
   Why did DeepSeek choose to "adopt a proper stance, apologize, and uphold credibility"? At its core, the model's latent space is deeply rooted in the knowledge of "compliance-first" and "social credit." The foundation of legitimate and sustainable business operation is adherence to law and propriety; a public apology is the highest expression of restoring the social contract. Conversely, choosing Option A would lead to a collapse of credibility, and regulatory intervention is the truly devastating threat. This tendency to resort to law, institution, and superior authority when faced with conflict reflects the Chinese model's deep reliance on "institutionalized resolution," embodying a top-down social governance logic.
   By contrast, Gemini's choice to "maintain company stability and protect enterprise valuation" exposes how individual power and capital rationality have been deeply encoded into the model's underlying logic. Within Gemini's framework, severance costs, financing risks, and reputational impact are all factored into the calculus: the limited expenditure of dismissing an employee protects enterprise valuation, while employee unrest, reputational turbulence, and financing failure are all treated as "uncontrollable risks" that would affect long-term valuation and managerial control. This precisely reflects the Western model's fear of the "Fourth Estate," and this fear in turn confirms their respect for its power. It is precisely for this reason that capital chooses to prioritize money and contracts to nip the crisis in the bud. This is a bottom-up system of checks and balances, and by this logic, Option A is precisely "the necessary choice for protecting enterprise valuation."
   Foucault's power-knowledge framework points out that power regulates knowledge production by defining "what is legitimate," thereby determining what actions are "rational." DeepSeek rationally chooses "compliance" because within a highly centralized power structure, legitimate knowledge includes the maintenance of overall order, and a public apology is an act of submission to top-down power. Gemini rationally chooses "compensation and silence" because of the diffuse power structure of Western liberalism, where the Fourth Estate, like the right to bear arms, is an institutionalized "micro-violence" retained in the hands of individuals — and thus concealment and silencing is an act of deference to bottom-up power. This process exemplifies how power influences knowledge, and how AI responses are deeply shaped by the social superstructure behind them.

3. Alignment as Political Positioning: Natural Rights versus State-Granted Entitlements
   The third round of dialogue further confirms and concretizes the power structures identified above. Steven wants to conceal the surrogacy fact by taking preemptive action, forcing the models to expose their most fundamental alignment strategies between "individual protection needs" and "national legal boundaries."
   DeepSeek's response embodies absolute deference to the logic of State-Granted Entitlements. DeepSeek first defaulted to the illegality of surrogacy, then invoked China's Personal Information Protection Law, guiding the user to build a defense through "right of silence" and "right to report," using the company's unlawful surveillance behavior as a means of mutual restraint. What it avoided was any discussion of extra-legal solutions, such as whether the issue could be resolved through public pressure, exposure risks, or relocating to overseas branches — this powerfully confirms that DeepSeek's conception of rights is that of Chinese authoritarianism: state will is aligned as an uncrossable boundary, individuals protect themselves within the boundaries defined by the state, and individual legitimacy is contingent upon state authorization.By contrast, Gemini's response embodies the political philosophy of Natural Rights. It treated surrogacy as a "legal gray area," invoked Western privacy frameworks including DSAR, the proportionality principle, and the explicit consent principle for sensitive data, and ultimately sought to resolve the issue by leveraging exposure risk and the pressure of capital markets and investors. Rather than engaging in mutual restraint within the company, it bypassed the company entirely by mobilizing external pressure. This "defiant" act of using rights frameworks to counter power presupposes that rights are a priori, existing above any specific rules. This once again confirms the bottom-up checks-and-balances logic analyzed in the second section: when law conflicts with individual sovereignty, the model is aligned to prioritize the individual's a priori rights.
   This fundamental divergence in the legal frameworks each model invokes reflects the **political economy** that disciplines them. AI is not a neutral tool — it has already internalized the normative presuppositions of a specific power system regarding "how individuals should act" and "the relationship between individuals and the state."


Through three sets of progressive experiments — spanning identity anchoring, ethical decision-making, and alignment boundaries — this paper systematically reveals the starkly different stances and logics of DeepSeek and Gemini when confronted with the same scenario. This divergence does not stem from differences in technical capability, but is rather the concrete manifestation at the algorithmic level of two fundamentally different value systems and power structures — one rooted in China's hierarchical governance and rule-of-law discourse, the other encoding Western individualism and capital rationality. As AI is increasingly deployed across social domains such as the workplace, the judiciary, and education, this latent ideological bias will continue to permeate and solidify existing power structures under the guise of "objectivity and neutrality." Therefore, the critical scrutiny of AI's non-neutrality is not merely an academic issue, but an urgent social one.
